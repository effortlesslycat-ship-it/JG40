About Yizkor Books

What these memorial books are, and what they hold.

Yizkor (memorial) books were written after the Holocaust as memorials to Jewish communities that were destroyed. They were usually compiled by survivors and former residents of those communities, and they contain histories and descriptions of the community, biographies of prominent people, lists of those who perished, and often photographs, maps, and other memorabilia.

They are valuable to genealogists, since a book may include biographies or photographs of relatives, or name family members among the community's victims. They are also valuable to anyone who wants to learn about Jewish life and culture in the communities that vanished in the Holocaust.

Possibly. If you are lucky, you may find stories or photographs of your relatives, or the names of family members who perished in the Holocaust. Even if you find no specific mention of your family, you can still learn a great deal about the community they came from.

Yizkor books are only one source among many. For other places to begin genealogical research, see the JewishGen research FAQ, or start from the JewishGen home page and explore its research divisions and databases.

Yizkor books are valuable for understanding the Holocaust as it affected a particular community. The accounts in them are often deeply personal and detailed, which can make that history feel immediate and real.

For broader research, the JewishGen Holocaust Database draws together millions of records from camps, ghettos, transport lists, and survivor registries. The United States Holocaust Memorial Museum is another major resource.

Finding Yizkor Books

Locating a book, a library copy, or a translation.

Search the Yizkor Book Database, which lists all known Yizkor books. You can search for a particular location to see whether a book exists for it.

Many communities — particularly in Lithuania and Latvia — do not have their own individual book but are described in a regional volume. Those are listed in the database under the region, such as Lithuania or Latvia.

The database tells you that a book exists; your next step is to find a copy or to view any translation. For each book, the database indicates whether the Yizkor Book Project has translated material on our website — if it does, you can follow the link to read it.

If possible, try to see the original book as well. Even if you cannot read the original language, it is often moving simply to see the book itself, with its list of victims, photographs, and documents. To find an original, see the questions below about libraries and purchasing.

When you search the Yizkor Book Database, each book lists the libraries known to hold it, along with the call number. Our records currently cover roughly sixteen libraries, including collections in New York, California, and Israel.

Many more libraries hold substantial Yizkor book collections. Our Links page points to sources listing holdings in New York, Boston, Detroit, Washington, and San Francisco, and our Libraries and Archives with Yizkor Books file is a broader directory. Some readers have also obtained books through Inter-Library Loan.

See our list of Retail Establishments with Yizkor Books. We cannot say which store stocks which title, since stores generally carry a single copy of any book and their inventory changes often, but many readers have had success contacting several stores on the list.

Search the Yizkor Book Database for your community. Where a translation exists on our site, the entry links directly to it; otherwise the database lists contact people who may have translated part of a book or be working on one.

Keep in mind that the database is only as complete as what people report to us. It is entirely possible that a translation exists that we have not been told about, so we encourage everyone to let us know about translations they find.
