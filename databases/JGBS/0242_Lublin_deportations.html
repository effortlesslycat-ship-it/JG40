Background

The Germans occupied the city of Lublin almost immediately upon entering it on September 18, 1939.  At the time, it was a city of approximately 122,000 people, and over 42,900 of them (35%) were Jewish.  During the initial takeover of the city, many young and politically active men left Lublin, and by the time the Nazis compiled their first registration of the Lublin Jews on October 25, 1939, there were 37,054 Jewish inhabitants recorded.

According to noted Lublin historian Robert Kuwalek, the Germans selected 14,000 Jews for deportation from Lublin to other nearby towns just prior to the establishment of the Lublin Ghetto in 1941.  Various lists of the people deported still exist in the Polish State Archives, and copies of them are held at the U.S. Holocaust Memorial Museum (USHMM) in Washington and Yad Vashem in Jerusalem.

This particular list is actually a collection of lists of Lublin Jews deported to other towns in the Lublin area between March 10-18, 1941 — exactly one year before the liquidation of the Lublin Ghetto.  It is catalogued at the Lublin branch of the Polish State Archives (Archiwum Państwowe w Lublinie) in the Judenrat collection as Sygnatura 153, entitled: "Jews 'Voluntarily' Deported from Lublin to Bełżyce, Chodel, Lubartów, Rejowiec, and Siedliszcze".  However, Mr. Kuwalek adds that it is well-known that these Lubliners did not volunteer, but were, in fact, forcibly removed from Lublin.  They were allowed to pack a suitcase but took no food or supplies with them.  There were no accommodations or provisions waiting for them at their assigned destinations and most of the Lubliners became refugees housed in synagogues and other communal buildings.  In other words, they were "dumped" in these smaller towns.  A copy of this deportation list was filed with the local Judenrat, and is so noted at the end of each individual list in this file.

Some families attempted to return to Lublin, only to find that other families had moved into their apartments while the deported families waited patiently for their reinstatement requests to be processed "legally".  Most of the people on these lists now shared the destiny of the local Jews in those towns, and some of these people are known to have survived the war.  Therefore, the individual fates of people on this list cannot be surmised by their presence on the list, and further research may be necessary, if possible, to trace individuals through the remainder of the war.  Approximately equal numbers of men and women appear on these deportation lists, and all age groups are fairly evenly represented, so the lists themselves do not seem to indicate how people were selected for deportation.

Database

This database includes the names of 4,120 Jews who were deported from Lublin to neighboring towns between March 10-18, 1941.  They appear generally in alphabetical order, by family, and generally with head of household first, followed by the wife if applicable, and then listed by children, usually in birth order.  There are an additional 44 people listed in alphabetical order as an addendum at the end of the list, and we have noted their presence in the addendum in the Notes column.  We have also changed and noted obvious corrections to original clerks' typos, where the intention of the original typist can be determined (for example, perpetuating the mistyped date of "1995" in a 1940-era document would serve no purpose, so this mistake is noted in the database).

The fields for this database are as follows:

  • Name (Surname + Given name)
  • Date of Birth / Age (when given)
  • Birthplace (when given)
  • Transport Date
  • Destination (town name: Bełżyce, Chodel, Lubartów, Rejowiec, or Siedliszcze)
  • List Entry Number
  • Notes (some from original document, some noting correction of typos)
  • Image # (microfilm frame number, listed as a jpg file)

Acknowledgments

The list of "Jews 'Voluntarily' Deported to Bełżyce, Chodel, Lubartów Rejowiec, and Siedliszcze" can be found in the Lublin Judenrat files, which are housed at the Lublin branch of the Polish State Archives (AP).  The file is labeled "Sygnatura 153" of Zespolu 891/0, and appears on microfilm # 118893 of the Lublin AP.  The U.S. Holocaust Memorial Museum has a copy of all this material in Washington, D.C., and an additional copy exists at Yad Vashem in Jerusalem.
