Moldavian Jewish Victims of Soviet Oppression

3,847 Records

Holocaust · Soviet Oppression · Bessarabia · Moldova

Background

This database documents the Soviet oppression of Jewish citizens of Moldova from 1937 to 1991, derived primarily from the book Cartea Memoriei (The Book of Memory) and expanded through archival research at the National Archives of the Republic of Moldova. Most oppression occurred during 1941–1951 and ended with exoneration or rehabilitation in 1988–1991 with the collapse of the Soviet Union.

According to the Ministry of National Security in 1991, the communist regime sentenced about 5,500 people to death, sent 34,000 to jail, and deported 25,000 families to Siberia and other locations primarily during the 1940s. The Black Book of Communism (1997) estimates the total number of deported Moldovans at 94,792 people.

Of the 71,636 names of oppressed people in the source materials, 3,847 are assumed to be Jewish. The relatively small number can be attributed to several factors: the two main waves of oppression occurred in 1941 and 1949, and by 1949 very few Jews remained in Moldova. Additionally, the war interrupted the Soviet deportation program before it could be completed.

The Two Phases
Phase 1: Cartea Memoriei (1997–2003)

The original Cartea Memoriei was launched by the National History Museum of Moldova in 1991, drawing on documentation from the National Archives, the Ministry of Internal Affairs, the Ministry of National Security, the General Prosecutor’s Office, and the Supreme Court of the Republic of Moldova.

Phase 2: National Archive Collections (2013–2025)

Since the publication of Cartea Memoriei, additional documents were made available by the National Archives of Moldova, including nominal lists of deportees by special trains (July 1949), collections of documents of expelled and deported persons, collections relating to political repressions (20s–90s of the 20th century), and documents of persons convicted by military courts (1920–1990).

By the end of 2025, 1,346 original records had been updated and 1,345 new records were added. The total stands at 4,750 records.

Research Notes
Reasons for Oppression

The Soviets used nearly any reason to deport or imprison someone. The main reasons targeting Jews included: being a merchant or store owner (40% of cases), belonging to a Zionist organization, being a kulak (wealthy farmer), being classified as a “dangerous social element,” belonging to “counterrevolutionary organizations,” and various forms of “collaboration.”

Places of Detention and Deportation

About 60% of Jews had information about where they were sent. Over 90% (of those with location data) were sent to the following regions: Tiumen (460+, western Siberia), Irkutsk (400+, southeast Siberia), Kurgan (380+, southern Russia), Tomsk (265+, southwest Siberia), Kazakhstan (200+), Kemerovo (125+, southwest Siberia), and Sverdlovsk/Ivdellag (110+, southern Russia).

Key Terms

Forced labor also translates as “hard labor.” Rehabilitated means having former privileges or reputation restored — many were rehabilitated posthumously in 1988–1991. Classified or Included in the List refers to Soviet categorization, primarily “merchants” (largely Jewish) and “kulaks” (largely non-Jewish).
