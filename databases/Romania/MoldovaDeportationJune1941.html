Mass Deportation from Moldova, June 1941

2,517 Records

Holocaust · Deportation · Bessarabia · Moldova

Background

This database contains information about 2,517 individuals deported from Moldova by the Soviet occupation authorities on June 13–16, 1941. The data was derived from a study of this mass deportation performed with the support of the Academy of Sciences of the Republic of Moldova Institute of State History and Law.

The book contains lists of people from several Moldovan districts who were sent to concentration camps (primarily family heads) and those deported to Kazakhstan and Siberia (primarily children, women, and older people). Despite the fact that some lists are lost and unlikely to be recovered, the data allowed the Academy to determine the national entities that suffered the most from these policies.

Among the categories targeted were “anti-Soviets” and “counterrevolutionary elements,” including landowners, industrialists, merchants, members of Romanian political parties, former policemen, and gendarmes. Apart from Romanians who predominate in the lists, Jewish minorities made up an important part — giving clear evidence of the plight of the Bessarabian Jews during the mass deportations of summer 1941.

Fields Recorded

Each record may contain the following fields (many tables had different columns, so some fields are often empty):

Relationship — Relationship between family members
Birth — Year of birth
Occupation — Occupation or profession
Home-District — District in which the person lived
Hometown/Village — Town or village
Address — Street address
Purpose of Table — The type of list the person appears in
Comments — Additional information about the person
Research Notes
Types of Lists in the Source

The book contains over 150 distinct tables, of which about 50 contain lists of names. Approximately 35 of these contain some or many Jews. The tables include lists of persons condemned to death, persons deported from different districts, major merchants and traders, dossiers submitted for investigation, persons registered as anti-Soviets or counterrevolutionaries, family heads sent to concentration camps, persons missed during the deportation, and persons whose foreign currency was confiscated.

Since many tables have overlapping purposes, the same named people are often found in several tables. The original information was documented by Soviet authorities in Russian Cyrillic; transliteration varied significantly, with the same person sometimes spelled very differently across tables.

Coverage by District

The database covers the following districts (with approximate Jewish record counts): Călăraşi (566), Soroca (336), Bender (250), Chişinău (159), Vertiujeni (118), Cahul (99), Bălţi (35), Teleneşti (27), Tiraspol (27), and others (46). An additional 854 records have no district specified.
