Bessarabia Business Directory, 1924

13,144 Records

Business Directory · Bessarabia · Moldova · Ukraine

Background

This database contains records of more than 13,000 grocers, teachers, local officials, stone carvers, butchers, bakers, innkeepers, lawyers, peddlers, rabbis, and many others who worked in the early 1920s in Romania’s eastern counties (formerly Bessarabia Gubernia of the Russian Empire), and who were apparently Jewish.

The information was extracted from the Anuarul “Socec” al României-mari [“Socec” Annuary of the Great-Roumania], published in Bucureşti in 1924–1925 by the prominent Romanian publishing house Socec & Co. The Library of Congress describes the two-volume set as a historic address book that “stands as the most complete survey of Greater Romania during the interwar period.”

About the publisher: Socec & Co. was founded in 1856 in Bucharest by Ion V. Socec, a Jewish bookseller who built his shop into the first bookstore chain in Romania, with 13 stores by 1939. The company published leading Romanian writers and printed textbooks. The business directory was intended as an annual publication, but its editions were based on data collected before 1923. Page images for the full directory are available via the Library of Congress European Reading Room. Bessarabia pages begin at image number 1913.
Coverage by County

The database covers 705 localities across nine counties that formed the Bessarabia region, plus Chişinău (Kishinev) separately. No Jewish names appeared in listings for many towns and villages. Research Notes
Finding Original Directory Images

The Library of Congress created page images for the entire printed directory and made them available online through its Digital Collections. A complete description of the directory, research suggestions, and a link to the page images is available at loc.gov/rr/european/phonero/romdir.html. Bessarabia pages begin at image number 1913. To find the pages for a specific county, refer to the LOC county guide.

After the breakup of the Soviet Union, the independent Republic of Moldova was formed from most of Bessarabia, while significant portions came under Ukrainian rule. Names have changed since 1924 for many towns and villages. The database shows the present name, county or province, and modern nation for each 1924 locality.
