Tiraspol Uezd Revision Lists (1796–1858)

8,941 Records

Revision Lists · Census · Bessarabia · Transnistria

Background

The Reviskie Skazki (Revision Lists) were introduced in the Russian Empire by Peter the Great to account for individuals and families subject to taxation and to identify men liable for military service. Ten major revisions were conducted, with the first in 1719. Only a few are applicable to Tiraspol Uezd, which emerged after the Treaty of Jassy (1791) when Russia acquired the region between the South Bug and Dniester rivers.

This project of translating and transcribing Tiraspol Uezd Revision Lists began in September 2015. The database covers records from Tiraspol (1796, 1858), Dubossary (1799, 1816, 1850, 1858), Grigoriopol (1850, 1858), and the Jewish agricultural colonies of Voynova, Buzinova, Balashova, and Kisilevka (all 1858).

Today, the former Tiraspol Uezd is in eastern Moldova and Odesa Oblast, Ukraine. During the tenth revision (1858–1859), the uezd accounted for 318 localities including Tiraspol, Grigoriopol, and Dubossary.

Fields Recorded

The fields are the same as the Bessarabia Revision Lists Database, with these differences: the Uyezd field contains “Tiraspol,” the Gubernia field contains “Kherson,” and the archive citation refers to the Kherson State Archive rather than MoldSA. The LDS Microfilm # field is not applicable.

Research Notes
Geo-Political History

Tiraspol Uezd changed administrative hands multiple times: part of Voznesensky Governorship (1795–1797), Novorossiya Governorate (1797–1802), Nikolaevsky Governorship (1802–1803), and finally Kherson Gubernia from 1806 onward. In 1990, a portion of the former uezd (including Tiraspol, Dubossary, and Grigoriopol) declared independence as the unrecognized “Pridnestrovian Moldavian Republic.”

Notes on Specific Towns

Tiraspol 1796: This revision list has no surnames but often provides information about where each family came from. Head of the Jewish Community was Mordka Davidovich. 463 records.

Dubossary 1799: No surnames, but some entries state the head of household’s occupation, which may have later become a surname. 486 records. Submitted by Shmil Dibinskoy and Leyba Varshavskiy.

Jewish Agricultural Colonies (1858): Four colonies were documented: Voynova (454 records), Buzinova (949 records, settled on the land of landlord De-Senek), Balashova (507 records, owned by Pereyaslavl merchant Vulf Nosinov), and Kisilevka (56 records, farmers settled on their own land).
