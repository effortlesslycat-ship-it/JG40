1898 Census of Podu Iloaiei

723 Records

Census · Romania · Moldova Region

Background

Podu Iloaiei (Podul-Iloaei), a small town in the Moldavian region of Romania about 17 miles west of Iaşi, does not appear on many maps — yet a book of 168 closely-printed pages about this town’s Jewish life was published in 1990, describing its history, local economy, cultural activities, synagogues, and rabbinical traditions. Of special interest to genealogists, the book also contains several censuses of the Jews living in this town, the earliest dated 1824 and the latest 1898.

It is the 1898 census, consisting of 723 names across 26 pages, which has been extracted, translated, and presented here. The census information was extracted from the Romanian State Archives in Iaşi by Dr. Itic Svart-Kara and published in his book Obştea evreiască din Podu Iloaiei: file din istoria unui “ştetl” moldovenesc (Bucureşti: Hasefer, 1990). The entire book has since been translated and is available on the JewishGen Yizkor Book site.

Translation history: The census was translated by the late Nat Abramowitz, z”l, with archaic occupation terms defined through a collaborative effort among ROM-SIG members. Sandi Goldsmith, Marcel and Mary Bratu, Prof. Ladislau Gyemant, Terry Lasky, Susanna Vendel, and Paula Zieselman all contributed to the definitions of Romanian occupations, now available as a Romanian-English occupations table.

Research Notes
Notes on the Data

The Comments column contains references to “Russian Subject” or “Austrian Subject.” These refer to individuals called Sudits — foreigners living in Romania during the 19th century under the diplomatic and legal protection of a foreign government, granted special economic privileges through treaties with the Sultan of the Ottoman Empire.

The census has a number of inconsistencies that cannot be fully explained. Some may have been misreadings by Dr. Kara from the original handwritten records. For example, some males are listed as being “vaduva” (widow, not widower), and two males aged 21 and 28 have “wives” listed as 52 and 60 — possibly their mothers.
