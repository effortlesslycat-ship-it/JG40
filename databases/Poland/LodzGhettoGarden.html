Garden of Stones

Zachor - Gedenk - Remember

The act of remembering is a sacred Jewish obligation.  Placing a small stone at the grave of one who has passed is a tradition that in its simplicity gives physical expression to memory.

But how do we possible remember 6,000,000 people, grandmothers, grandfathers, mothers, fathers, sisters, brothers, daughters, sons and babies - most of whom have no grave on which to place our stones.

My name is Ann Oster (Chana Weisleder Ostrowiecki) and I am the daughter and daughter-in-law of Holocaust survivors.

My parents, Solomon and Doris Weisleder of Tomaszow Lubelski, survived the labor campls of Siberia, and my in-laws Bernard and Miriam Oster survived the Lodz Ghetto and several concentration camps. However, their 3 1/2 year old daughter Jocheved and 1 1/2 year old son Avrum were taken from them in the Lodz Ghetto and gassed in Chelmno in September 1942.

Here I am, in September 2003, with no pictures of Avrum and Jocheved and no grave to visit to shed the tears that fill my eyes as I imagine these two beautiful innocent Jewish children and the incomprehensibility of their brutal murder.

As I stand here in this Garden of Stones, so brilliantly conceived by Andy Goldsworthy, I am struck by the powerful emotion it evokes in me personally. For me, these 18 hollowed boulders not only symbolize the resilience of life in a most barren environment, but the infinite strength and conviction of our Jewish collective memory that sustains us as a people. For me they are the symbol of the infinite number of stones we and future generations must place on the graves that exist only in our hearts and minds. The waters of the New York harbor that flow beyond us are for me the infinite tears we shed for our Kedoshim.

I was privileged and honored yesterday to plant an oak sapling in one of the boulders with my husband Abe, my son Avi, and my dear mother, Doris - a Survivor. I was so touched as I watched my mother's reaction as she approached the garden for the first time. She was jarred, she stared, she stood so still. And then as we planted this small tree, she placed her hands on the boulder so delicately and closed her eyes and cried. She told me that she felt that she now had a Place.
