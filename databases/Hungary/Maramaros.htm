Background

The Maramures (formerly Máramaros) County Jewish Records Indexing Project is a volunteer-run genealogy and history project, working to create a free searchable online database of all known surviving Jewish vital records — birth, marriage and death records of the mid-19th thru mid-20th centuries — from the former Hungarian county (megye) of Máramaros.

Máramaros County (in Yiddish: מאַרמעראָש, Marmerosh) is no longer in Hungary — After the First World War (via the 1920 Treaty of Trianon), the northern part of the county became part of newly formed nation of Czechoslovakia, and the southern part became part of Romania. After WWII, the formerly Czechoslovak part of Máramaros became part of the Soviet Union. Today, the area that was formerly Máramaros County is split by the Tisza River between two countries—the southern part is included in Maramureş judeţ (county) of northern Romania, and the northern half is in Zakarpats'ka oblast (Закарпатська область = Sub-Carpathian Province) of southwestern Ukraine.

Beginning in 1851, the government of the Kingdom of Hungary required each religious community to record vital records (i.e.: births, marriages, and deaths). Thus, most of the towns in Máramaros County recorded Jewish vital records beginning in the 1850s. Civil Registration — meaning that everyone in a given town was registered in the same book, regardless of their religion — began in 1895.

This project initially focused only on the Jewish community vital records, which were the official records from 1851 to October 1895. These records have now been fully transcribed in this database (other than some late-found registers which are awaiting fo transcription.) In 2014, we began extracting Jewish records from the Civil Registers, which begin in October 1895.

The surviving birth, marriage, and death register books for the Jewish communities of Máramaros County are currently stored at a regional branch of the Romanian National Archives (Arhivele Naţionale ale României), located in the city of Baia Mare, Romania.

We continue to obtain digital photographs of the Jewish community record books as they become available. The cost of obtaining the records is supported by generous contributions from our interested donors. Other than actually traveling to the archives in Baia Mare, or hiring a researcher to go there, the only way for genealogists and historians to research these records is through this project which is managed through the JewishGen Hungarian Research Division (formerly H-SIG). Currently, the coordinator of the project is Sandy Malek, under the leadership of the Hungarian Research Division Director, Vivian Kahn. Dena Whitman has been assisting the effort.

Information in the Original Data

Generally speaking, early records in this data set — that is, records from the 1850s-1860s — have only bare minimum information available, whereas the later records from the very end of the 19th century have far more information available.  For example, a typical birth records set from the 1880s or 1890s would usually have all of these types of information available, and perhaps more:

  • Child's first name
  • Child's date of birth
  • Child's gender
  • Father's surname
  • Father's first name
  • Father's hometown
  • Father's occupation
  • Mother's maiden name
  • Mother's first name
  • Mother's hometown
  • Sandek/Godfather's name [who was quite often a relative, such as a grandfather or uncle]
  • Sandek/Godfather's hometown
  • Town of birth (possibly with street address of birth and/or residence)
  • Town of registration of birth (usually the same as "Town of Birth", but not always).
  • Notes found in the original record's margins, and remarks by the transcriber about the condition of the record
  • Other surnames mentioned in this record
  • Other towns mentioned in this record

Important note: In the death records for this database, married women who died were sometimes recorded with their married surname, and sometimes with their maiden surname.  Not every record clearly indicates whether the record lists the woman’s maiden name or married name.

Post-1906 records from the larger towns, such as Sighet, also include information such as the address of residence (valuable in the event of a planned visit to ancestral towns) as well as occupations and causes of death. Some records include information from the 1930s through the 1960s, which are included in the notes made regarding later events.
