1. About the JGFF

The JewishGen Family Finder (JGFF) is a database of surnames and towns currently being researched by Jewish genealogists worldwide. It contains ancestral surnames and town names, and is indexed and cross-referenced by both surname and town name.

The JGFF is a networking tool, designed to connect genealogists with similar research interests. Its purpose is to allow people who are researching the same surnames and towns to discover each other, and then share information. All Jewish genealogists are encouraged to participate.

The JGFF contains names of ancestral towns and ancestral surnames, submitted by people around the world who are researching their Jewish family roots.

Each entry in the JGFF database consists of three components:

  • The Surname being researched
  • The Town being researched (town name and country name)
  • The researcher's JewishGen ID Number

The JewishGen ID Number is a reference to the researcher who submitted the surname and town name. If you find something of interest, you can contact that researcher for more information.

Here is a sample entry:

SurnameTownCountry Researcher (JewishGen ID Number)
CohenMinskBelarus Joe User (#1234)
27 Yichus Drive
Youngstown, OH 12345
Click HERE to contact Joe User (#1234)

The above sample entry indicates that Joe User (JewishGen ID #1234) is interested in the surname Cohen from the town of Minsk, Belarus. If you are interested in the same town and/or surname, you can write to Joe User via the provided postal address or email link, and exchange information.

The JGFF does not contain information about individuals. It contains no given names, nor any dates.

The JGFF data comes from you, the participants.

The JGFF consists of surnames and town names of personal interest, submitted by people researching their own Jewish family history. It is not collected from any other source.

In April 1982, the New York-based Jewish Genealogical Society published a 37-page typewritten roster of ancestral towns and surnames being researched by 83 members of the society. This was the genesis of what became the “Jewish Genealogical Family Finder.” Gary Mokotoff, a member of the society, computerized the list and expanded the JGFF by permitting contributions from any person researching their Jewish ancestry, even if not a member of the New York group. Mokotoff maintained the JGFF under the auspices of the Jewish Genealogical Society, Inc. from 1982 until 1996, turning over profits to the society. Printed copies were distributed to Jewish Genealogical Societies throughout the world semi-annually, and the JGFF was also made available on microfiche.

In 1991, the JGFF was made available on several dial-up computer bulletin boards (BBSs), in a project developed by Susan King. In 1994, it was made available on the Internet via a World Wide Web and email retrieval system developed by Warren Blatt for JewishGen. In 1996, a new online system was developed by Michael Tobias, which included the ability to enter and modify entries online.

In 1996, ownership of the JGFF was transferred from the Jewish Genealogical Society (New York) to JewishGen, Inc., a not-for-profit corporation, to make the data more widely available. It was renamed the “JewishGen Family Finder.”

There are no costs for use of the JGFF. Everyone may participate, free of charge. The JGFF is maintained by JewishGen, Inc., a not-for-profit corporation.

However, while JewishGen is completely free of cost to users, there are real costs to administer and maintain the database. 2. What the JGFF Is Not

The JGFF is not a database of individuals. The JGFF is not a database of families. It does not contain any information about people, places, or times. The JGFF is not a missing persons service. It does not contain data collected from any historical sources.

The JGFF is a database of only surnames and towns being researched by Jewish genealogists worldwide, nothing more. It is a purely contributory database, containing only information submitted by you, the users.

The purpose of the JGFF is to allow people with common research interests to find each other, so they can then share information.

3. How to Search

Go to the JGFF and select the Search button. You will be presented with a form where you can enter a surname and/or town name, filter by country, and choose a match type. Fill in the form and press “Search.”

The search form offers four types of name matching for both surnames and towns:

  • Is Exactly — matches exact spelling only
  • Starts With — matches based on a prefix
  • Sounds Like — matches using the Daitch-Mokotoff Soundex system
  • Contains — matches text found anywhere within a name

“Is Exactly” searching: Finds only exact spelling matches. For surnames, this is rarely useful, since Jewish surnames were inconsistently spelled in historical records. For town names with a specific country selected, this option can also draw on the town synonym database.

“Starts With” searching: Finds all names beginning with your entered text. For example, “Stein” matches “Stein”, “Steinberg”, “Steiner”, “Steinman”, etc.

“Sounds Like” searching: Finds all similar-sounding names using the Daitch-Mokotoff Soundex system. For example, a search for “Lvov” will also find “Lebow” and “Lubawa”; a search for “Rosensteen” will also find “Rosenstein”, “Rozenstine”, and “Rouzinsztejn.” See the JewishGen Soundex InfoFile for more information about the Daitch-Mokotoff Soundex system.

“Contains” searching: Finds all records containing your text anywhere within the name field. For example, “Blatt” matches “Blatt”, “Blattman”, “Feinblatt”, “Greenblatt”, “Steinblatter”, etc.

Your search results will show a list of people researching the same surnames and/or towns of interest to you. Here is a sample entry:

SurnameTownCountry Researcher (JewishGen ID Number)
CohenMinskBelarus Joe User (#1234)
27 Yichus Drive
Youngstown, OH 12345
Click HERE to contact Joe User (#1234)

This entry indicates that Joe User (JGFF researcher #1234) is interested in “Cohen” from “Minsk, Belarus.” If you share that interest, write via the postal address or email link, describe what you have in common, and offer to exchange information. (See Question 3.2.)

If you find several researchers interested in the same town or shtetl, you might also consider creating a KehilaLinks page for that community.

Contact researchers by writing to them via email or postal mail, depending on which they have chosen to list. To contact a researcher by email, click the “Click HERE” link in the search results, which opens the “Contact JGFF Submitter” form. (See Question 3.4.)

Tell the researcher who you are, how you found their listing, and what surnames and towns you share an interest in. Include dates and places. For example:

To: Joe User
From: Marci Cohen
Subject: Finkielstein family from Lublin and Philadelphia

I am researching the Finkielstein family from Lublin, Poland, and I found your name in the JewishGen Family Finder, which said that you are also researching Finkielsteins.

My grandparents were Abraham and Mollie Finkielstein, who came to Philadelphia in about 1910. Their children were Morris (Moshe), Hyman (Chaim), Peggy (Rivka) and Florence (Fayga), born in 1904, 1906, 1911 and 1914. Are you related to these Finkielsteins?

Sincerely,
Marci Cohen
Pittsburgh, PA

When writing by postal mail, be sure to include a self-addressed stamped envelope.

The JGFF has been running since 1982, and some legacy entries — particularly those submitted in the early years of the database — include only a postal address. Some participants have also simply chosen to be contacted by mail rather than email.

In those cases, you can write to them by postal mail: a letter, an envelope, and a stamp. Be sure to include a self-addressed stamped envelope with your letter.

JewishGen's “Blind Contact” system lets JGFF registrants maintain their anonymity. Registrants can choose to be listed with only their JewishGen ID Number displayed — no name, no address. In that case, search results show only: “Click HERE to contact Researcher #123456.”

Clicking that link opens a secure message form where you compose your message. JewishGen sends it on your behalf to the registrant, screening messages first to ensure they relate to personal genealogical research. This protects registrants from spam and unsolicited commercial contact.

Each JGFF registrant controls their own display preference. See Question 4.5 for the available display options, and Question 5 for how to change your preference.

The JGFFAlert System is a value-added service that constantly monitors all entries and changes made to the JGFF, and immediately notifies participants when a new entry is a potential match with one of their own. It relieves you of the need to periodically check for new entries — JewishGen does it for you.

Whenever a new surname/town record is added or edited, the system searches for matches. A “match” is defined as a record where the Surname matches by Daitch-Mokotoff Soundex, and the Town and Country match by exact spelling. Records where the town or country is blank or “Any” are never matched, so be specific in your own entries to take full advantage of this system.

When a match is found and the matching researcher is a JGFFAlert participant, they receive an immediate email with details about the new or edited record and a direct link to the other researcher's contact information.

To participate: The JGFFAlert System is available as a special thank-you to all who contribute a minimum of $100 annually to the JewishGen General Fund. Donations to hosted organizations or to Research Division projects do not qualify. Visit the Value-Added Services page to contribute.

4. How to Submit Your Data

Go to the JGFF and select the Enter button. If you are not already registered with JewishGen and logged in, you will be asked to do so.

Registration asks for your contact information: your name, address, email, etc. Please enter accurate information to enable other researchers to contact you. You also select your privacy display options during registration (see Question 4.5).

Once registration is complete, you will proceed to the data entry screen to enter your ancestral surname and town listings. You may enter up to 16 entries per screen. To add more later, see Question 5.1.

A JGFF entry consists of one surname associated with one town within one country. If you want to enter variant spellings, create a separate entry for each:

Cohen            Warszawa      Poland    <-- CORRECT
Kahn             Warszawa      Poland    <-- CORRECT
Cohen or Kahn    Warszawa      Poland    <-- INCORRECT

If you are researching a surname from two different localities, create two separate entries:

Schwartz         Grodno        Belarus   <-- CORRECT
Schwartz         Bialystok     Poland    <-- CORRECT
Schwartz         Grodno/Bialystok          <-- INCORRECT

Use only the 26 letters of the Latin alphabet. Do not use slashes, dashes, parentheses, brackets, commas, numbers, or accented characters.

Spelling variants: You typically do not need to enter all variant spellings of a surname. The Daitch-Mokotoff Soundex search finds them automatically — “Meyerson”, “Meirzon”, and “Majersohn” will all be found if someone searches for “Maiersen.”

The JGFF uses the modern contemporary name of each town. Many cities and towns, especially in Eastern Europe, have had alternative names or spellings due to political and linguistic changes over time. The JGFF uses the name of the locality as it is known today.

For example, “Lviv, Ukraine” was formerly known as “Lemberg, Austria” before World War I, then “Lwów, Poland” between the wars, then “L'vov, U.S.S.R.” after WWII. The JGFF always uses the modern name: Lviv, Ukraine.

Town names must be in the native official spelling (Roman alphabet), not the Anglicized version. The capital of Poland is entered as “Warszawa,” not “Warsaw.” Omit any accents or diacritical marks. Some examples:

Warszawanot Warsaw (Poland)
Vilniusnot Vilna (Lithuania)
Kaunasnot Kovno (Lithuania)
Wiennot Vienna (Austria)
Prahanot Prague (Czech Republic)
Kyyivnot Kiev (Ukraine)
Munchennot Munich (Germany)
Gdansknot Danzig (Poland)
Bucurestinot Bucharest (Romania)
Kolnnot Cologne (Germany)

The best source for identifying the correct modern name for a Jewish ancestral town is the JewishGen Communities Database. Use only the bolded version of the town name in the search results.

Country names: Enter the country in which the town is located today.

Towns in the USA and Canada: Include the state or province using its official two-letter postal abbreviation, separated from the town by a comma and space, then enter “USA” or “Canada” as the country. Examples: “New York, NY”  |  “Chicago, IL”  |  “Toronto, ON.” Do not spell out the state name, and do not omit it.

Unknown locality: If you know the country but not the specific town, use the word “Any” in the Town field (e.g., “Any, Poland”). The use of “Any” is strongly discouraged — be as specific as possible for best results.

Characters: Use only the 26 letters of the Latin alphabet. Do not use slashes, dashes, hyphens, parentheses, brackets, or numbers. For hyphenated names, use spaces: “Frankfurt am Main”, not “Frankfurt-am-Main.” The JGFF does not support accented letters — enter “Koln” not “Köln”, “Munchen” not “München.”

While there is no system-imposed maximum, we strongly suggest limiting your entries to the major surnames you are directly researching — only your direct ancestral lines. Do not enter everyone on your family tree; that is not the purpose of the JGFF. For family tree data, see the FTJP (Question 4.4).

Typically, your entries should cover the surnames and ancestral towns of your four grandparents, eight great-grandparents, sixteen great-great-grandparents, and so on through your direct lines.

JewishGen respects the privacy of its users and never shares user addresses with any outside organization. To balance public listing with privacy, three “Display Options” are available:

5. How to Modify Your Entries

To modify or add to your existing entries, log in to JewishGen using your email address and password.

  • To modify your contact information (name, address, email, display options, or password), visit your JewishGen account settings.

  • To modify your surname/town entries, go to the JGFF and select the Modify button. You will have two options:

    • To modify existing entries, select “Modify Surname/Town Information.”
    • To add new entries, select “Add Additional Surname/Town Information.”
    Press “Send Request” and follow the instructions.

Go to the JGFF and select the Enter button. On the next screen, select “Add Additional Surname/Town Information” and press “Send Request.” Follow the instructions on the next page.

You create your own password when you initially register with JewishGen. JewishGen uses a password for each user to ensure that no one else can modify your data. Your password is set during the registration process the first time you use the Enter feature (see Question 4).

If you have forgotten your password, you can use the password reset screen to create a new one.

Visit your JewishGen account settings to change your password.

Visit your JewishGen account settings to update your address.

Visit your JewishGen account settings to update your email address.

Please note that changing your email address in JewishGen also updates it for the JewishGen Discussion Group. If you wish to use a different email address for your Discussion Group subscription, see the JewishGen Support Center.

Go to the JGFF, click Modify, select “Modify Surname/Town Information,” and press “Send Request.” Make your changes and press “Submit.”

To delete an entry, overwrite the surname with the word DELETE and submit. See Question 5.10.

The system automatically converts some common variants — for example, “Warsaw” is converted to “Warszawa” — using an internal town synonym database. The database is also periodically reviewed to correct entries that do not conform to JGFF standards. Please review Question 4.2.

If you believe a conversion on one of your entries is incorrect, contact the JGFF editor using the Town Query Form.

6. How to View Your Entries

Go to the JGFF and select the List button. If you are not already logged in, you will be prompted to do so. You can choose table output (better on screen) or plain text output (easier to copy into an email or word processor). Press “List Details” to view your entries.

7. General Research Help

By doing genealogical research! The JGFF is not a genealogical database — it is a networking tool for connecting researchers with similar interests. It lets others know what surnames and towns you are researching, and lets you discover what others are researching.

Finding information about your family requires your own research effort. Information about your ancestors is waiting to be uncovered in census records, passenger manifests, naturalization records, city directories, vital records, and more.

For guidance on where to begin, see the JewishGen FAQ: Frequently Asked Questions About Jewish Genealogy.

You can also post queries and connect with other researchers through the JewishGen Discussion Group.

8. Reporting Problems
